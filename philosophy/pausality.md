
# Pausality
## Relational Satisfaction As A Fecund Revision to Process Metaphysics

Ryan Hamel  
Montreal, QC  
March 07, 2026  


### Abstract
Alfred North Whitehead's process philosophy provides the most developed relational ontology in the Western tradition, yet nearly a century of process thought have largely declined to use it for its most ambitious purpose: a unified account of the physical world. This paper, the first in a series of papers, takes up that project. This paper defines *pausality*: concrescence cannot reach satisfaction without a downstream occasion creating the relational demand for its completion, providing a trigger mechanism for the transition from indeterminate to determinate. I call this pausality — the term reflects how the process appears from within spacetime. From the occasion's own perspective, the more precise term is relational satisfaction. The terms will be used interchangeably.


### Introduction
Whitehead intended process philosophy to be a cosmology — a comprehensive account of the nature of reality that would encompass physics, biology, consciousness, and value within a single metaphysical scheme. *Process and Reality* is explicit about this ambition. Yet the subsequent tradition has largely pursued Whitehead's insights in theology, ecology, and philosophy of mind while leaving the physics on the table[^1]. This is understandable. Whitehead's own engagement with physics was shaped by the science of the 1920s. The measurement problem had not yet matured into its modern form. Bell's theorem was decades away. Whitehead had the metaphysical primitives but lacked the physical phenomena that would most clearly demonstrate their power.

This paper attempts to pick up where Whitehead's physics left off. I propose that his core primitive — prehension, the process by which one actual occasion takes account of another — is sufficient to give an ontological account of the transition from indeterminate to determinate (the measurement problem). In the next paper, I will discuss how the same primitive is sufficient to ground a unified ontological account of not just the measurement problem, but also an ontology for time, time dilation, and gravity. The measurement problem is a large enough surface area to tackle in one paper, but the core move I am making has the fecundity to produce ontological accounts of previously unexplained universal phenomena. The core claim is that concrescence cannot close without relational demand — and that this single revision generates a trigger condition for quantum determination, dissolves the measurement problem, and provides structural accounts of entanglement and wave-particle duality.

Process philosophy, if it is true, applies to itself. *Process and Reality* is not an eternal object — it is an occasion in a historical nexus. As Isabelle Stengers has argued[^2], the point is to think *with* Whitehead, not to treat his system as a closed text requiring only exegesis. The revisions proposed here are substantial: a relational account of satisfaction that revises Whitehead's privacy/publicity distinction, and a reconception of God's consequent nature that makes divine prehension structurally redundant. 

A process philosophy that cannot be prehended and transformed by its successors has become the very thing it argues against — a static form, immune to novelty. This paper, and those that follow, proceeds in the spirit Whitehead himself described: the many become one, and are increased by one.


### 1.1 The Two Species of Process

Whitehead distinguishes two species of process: concrescence and transition. Concrescence is the internal becoming of an actual occasion — the process by which an occasion prehends its world, integrates what it receives, and arrives at a determinate satisfaction. Transition is the passage from one completed occasion into the data of another — the process by which a perished occasion becomes objective datum for subsequent occasions. These are not sequential phases where one is primary and the other derivative. Whitehead treats them as mutually necessary, co-fundamental aspects of how reality advances.

### 1.2 Transition Requires a Receiver

Transition is not an occasion broadcasting into the void. It is the process by which a perished occasion becomes objective datum *for* a subsequent occasion. The "for" is constitutive, not incidental. In a relational ontology, a datum that is datum for nothing is not a datum. Without a prehending occasion, transition has no content. This is not a controversial addition to Whitehead — it follows directly from the relational character of his metaphysics.

### 1.3 Concrescence Cannot Close Unilaterally

Here is the central claim. If transition is co-fundamental with concrescence, and transition requires a receiver, then an occasion that reaches satisfaction with no downstream occasion to prehend the result has undergone concrescence without transition — only half of process. The occasion is not suspended in some liminal state, nor is it waiting in ontological quarantine. It is fully underway: concrescing, prehending, integrating, exercising full autonomy over its internal process. What it cannot do is *complete*. Satisfaction — the moment of determinateness and perishing into objective datum — is contingent on there being a relational context for that completion. Concrescence has genuine duration, and that duration extends until a downstream occasion creates the demand for the result.

Consider the alternative. If an occasion could reach satisfaction without any downstream occasion to inherit the result, its determinateness would make no relational difference. In a relational ontology, determinateness that produces no possible relational difference is indistinguishable from indeterminateness.

**The occasion does not decide it is done. It is done when something needs it to be done.**

I call this *pausality*. A clarification on the word itself. *Pausality* is an emergent description, not an ontological claim about the occasion's own experience. From the occasion's perspective, nothing pauses — it undergoes exactly as many prehensions as it undergoes. The "pause" only appears from a vantage point that assumes temporal homogeneity across regions — what I call *time arrogance*, the assumption that all regions of the universe have experienced the same number of occasions. An occasion that has not yet entered into downstream relation is not frozen. It simply has not yet been inherited forward. Put most precisely: an occasion is always in relation to another occasion — the question is whether it is in relation to a *downstream* occasion. It is constituted by its antecedent relations; it is just not yet constitutive of subsequent ones.

A further distinction is necessary. The downstream occasion's role is to trigger the completion of concrescence — to create the relational demand that allows satisfaction. Prehension, meanwhile, accumulates freely throughout the occasion's duration without constraint. The occasion is not waiting in an empty room. It is mid-process, gathering and integrating prehensions, with its internal richness growing. What it lacks is the condition for closure. The trigger and the prehensive accumulation are independent.

### 1.3.1 On Retrocausality And Public vs Private
An objection arises: in Whitehead's system, an occasion is private while concrescing and becomes public only at satisfaction. How then can a downstream occasion prehend an unsatisfied occasion? The answer requires revising the privacy/publicity distinction itself. If satisfaction is relational rather than unilateral, then the sharp boundary between private concrescence and public datum softens. An unsatisfied occasion is not hidden from the universe. It is mid-process, constituted by antecedent relations, accumulating prehensions — it is relationally present. A downstream occasion does not read the unsatisfied occasion's subjective content. It enters into relation with an active site of concrescence. That relation is the trigger. The downstream occasion does not need the unsatisfied occasion to be finished in order to relate to it. It needs only to prehend it — and prehension, in a relational ontology, is not retrieval of a completed datum but the act of entering into relation. The demand is not a signal from the future. It is the present act of a new occasion reaching toward what is relationally available.

### 1.4 Empirical Consistency

The distinction between a trigger that resolves and prehension that accumulates is not merely conceptual. In the famous Bell tests, a quantum state is created in superposition — the indeterminate state — and only reaches a determinate state upon measurement. A Bell test, however, could still be conceived of metaphysically as a 'frozen' collection of occasions that only 'unfreeze' when needed. A more complete example are spin echo experiments. In a spin echo experiment, a quantum system appears to lose coherence — its phase information seemingly dispersed — only for a refocusing pulse to recover the coherent state, demonstrating that the system was accumulating and maintaining internal structure throughout the period of apparent indeterminacy. Whatever metaphysics one brings to such experiments, any account of concrescence must be consistent with this kind of physical behaviour: systems that retain and build internal richness through apparent indeterminacy, completed by a downstream interaction. Had Whitehead had access to spin echo experiments alongside the Bell tests that were still decades away, the path from his relational ontology to demand-driven concrescence would have been considerably shorter.

### 1.5 God's Consequent Nature and the Primordial Nature

Whitehead introduced God's consequent nature in part to guarantee that no occasion perishes without being taken up — that the universe is, in a sense, lossless. Every occasion that perishes is prehended by God, preserving its contribution. This is elegant, but if concrescence itself cannot complete without a downstream prehension partner, the problem God's consequent nature was introduced to solve does not arise. There are no orphaned occasions to rescue. The relational structure of process prevents them from occurring in the first place.

I want to be explicit that this is a significant revision of Whitehead, not a strict derivation from his premises. Whitehead did not make this move, and I am not claiming he was secretly committed to it. What I am claiming is that the revision cuts toward his core commitments rather than away from them. In a relational ontology, occasions cannot close unilaterally; satisfaction is constitutively social. Whitehead lacked the measurement problem in its modern form — the question of what triggers the transition from indeterminate to determinate had not yet crystallized into the shape that makes pausality visible as a candidate answer. This is a historical limitation, not a gap in the coherence of his system. Whitehead was doing a specific job: reconciling metaphysics with the physics of his moment. It has been nearly a century. The aim here is to prehend what that century has produced — across physics, the sciences, and the broader project of understanding what reality is — and let that inheritance drive process theory toward a new concrescence.

### 1.6 Pausality and Measurement

Whitehead describes concrescence as reaching "satisfaction" through its own internal process but does not specify what determines *when* satisfaction occurs. In standard quantum mechanics, an indeterminate system becomes determinate upon measurement, but the theory does not say what constitutes a measurement or why it has this effect. Pausality provides a unified answer: an occasion reaches satisfaction when a downstream occasion requires it as prehension. Determination is demand-driven. Measurement is not a special category of interaction, and observation is not metaphysically privileged. Any occasion that creates relational demand for the result of another occasion's concrescence triggers its completion. This dissolves the measurement problem rather than solving it — the problem was generated by treating determination as something that happens *to* a system from outside, when it is in fact the natural completion of a relational process.

The account offered here does not map cleanly onto any existing interpretation of quantum mechanics. It shares with Rovelli's relational quantum mechanics the conviction that determination is relational rather than absolute[^3], but differs in providing a specific trigger condition — relational demand from a downstream occasion — that relational QM leaves unspecified. It shares with causal realism the commitment to a real causal structure independent of observers. It is incompatible with interpretations that deny a unique outcome (many-worlds) or that treat quantum states as subjective (QBism). If pausality is correct, it constitutes a distinct interpretation: determination is neither collapse imposed by observation, nor branching into parallel outcomes, nor subjective updating of beliefs. It is the natural completion of a relational process.[^4]

### 1.7 Entanglement and the No-Signaling Constraint

Pausality has a further implication that deserves separate treatment. The no-signaling theorem in quantum mechanics — the result that entanglement cannot be used for faster-than-light communication — is well established formally. The reduced density matrix of either subsystem is unchanged by a distant measurement; the math is clear. What the formalism does not provide is an ontological account of *why* the universe is structured this way. Pausality offers one.

Consider a standard Bell test. Two entangled particles are separated and measured at distant locations. The standard account says their correlations are non-local but cannot be exploited for signaling. The question is why. The usual answer appeals to the formalism: the statistics at each wing are locally random regardless of what happens at the other. This is correct but ontologically silent. It describes the constraint without explaining its source.

Within pausality, entanglement is not best understood as two separate particles that happen to be correlated. It is an occasion[^5] that has constituted both wings without yet reaching satisfaction. The two wings are relational neighbours not because they are spatially proximate but because they share an unsatisfied occasion. Their "non-locality" is a spatial category error: occasions are individuated relationally, not geometrically. 

A note on the relational nature of the quantum occasion with the two wings - each of the two wings can be envisioned as potential relational partners, one might have said suitors in Whitehead's time, to be the downstream occasion that triggers satisfaction. That is, the potential for a relationship between them already exists, but forming the relationship requires consent from both the conscrescing occasion and the downstream occasion. Once satisfaction is triggered by one wing, the other wing simply inherits the result as prehension from the now-satisfied occasion. It does not trigger a second satisfaction. It receives a datum, as any occasion does from a satisfied occasion in its prehensive field. The correlation between the wings is not transmitted between them — it is inherited from a common parent.

When measurement occurs, the occasion is satisfied. Both wings are at causal distance 1 from that occasion — they are direct inheritors, not recipients of a traveling signal. There is no propagation event. Satisfaction is not something that moves from one wing to the other at infinite speed; there is no *it* that moves. The occasion was constituting both wings throughout; its satisfaction is a relational fact for both at once. Not instantaneous transfer — structurally non-sequential.

The no-signaling result then falls out from the structure of occasions. Signaling requires a directed causal chain: A influences B influences C. What entanglement produces is a fork — two occasions each at causal distance 1 from the same satisfied parent, neither downstream of the other. There is no directed path between the wings along which a signal could travel. Once the shared occasion is satisfied, the relationship that made the wings neighbours has perished. The occasion can only give prehension to non-satisfied occasions — it cannot reach backward or form new relationships with already-completed occasions. To establish a new causal connection between the wings, you would need new relational contact, which is just ordinary classical causation subject to ordinary spatial constraints.

The appearance of paradox is, once again, *time arrogance*: the assumption that the two wings occupy a shared temporal sequence and that the measurement result must somehow "catch up" from one to the other. They do not share a temporal sequence. They share a relational structure — the unsatisfied occasion that constituted both. Once that occasion is satisfied and perishes, the wings are genuinely separated. New occasions, no inherited relationship, nothing to exploit. The non-locality in spacetime was real but singular: one unsatisfied occasion, one satisfaction, one fork. It is a one-time relational fact — local in causal terms, non-local only from the perspective of spacetime. It is not a channel that persists. It is worth stressing - as far as both of the wings are concerned, the quantum occasion is a local relational fact whose potential relationship was formed at the moment of entanglement - when both wings were, in fact, local in spacetime.

### 1.8 Wave-Particle Duality

Pausality also dissolves wave-particle duality — not by explaining how something can be both wave and particle, but by showing that the distinction was never ontological in the first place.

An unsatisfied occasion has an expanding set of potential downstream occasions — possible relational partners that could trigger its satisfaction. This set is not a thing in space. It is the relational possibilities available to the occasion. As the occasion persists without satisfaction, nearby occasions continue to reach their own satisfaction and new occasions emerge, so the set of potential partners keeps shifting and expanding. From a spacetime perspective, this expanding set of potential relations *is* wave behaviour. The occasion is not "in many places at once." It has many potential relational partners at once.

When one of those potential partners commits — when a downstream occasion creates the relational demand for the result — satisfaction occurs. The occasion becomes determinate at a specific point. From a spacetime perspective, this is particle behaviour. Post-satisfaction, the next occasion inherits a constrained relational trajectory: the partners available to it are shaped by the specific satisfaction that just occurred. A measured photon travels in a straight line because its inherited relational context constrains its available downstream occasions to a narrow corridor.

Interference patterns follow from the same structure. In a double-slit experiment, the unsatisfied occasion's potential downstream partners include occasions reachable through both slits. Where potential relations from two paths converge, relational density is higher — more potential partners, greater likelihood of satisfaction occurring. Where they destructively overlap, fewer potential relations, lower likelihood. The pattern on the screen is not a wave hitting a wall. It is a map of relational density — the distribution of where satisfaction is most likely to occur given the available relational landscape.

The wave and the particle were never two modes of a single entity. The wave is what an unsatisfied occasion looks like from a spacetime perspective — an open field of potential relations. The particle is what a satisfied occasion looks like — a determinate relational event. The mystery of wave-particle duality was generated by assuming that both descriptions referred to the same kind of thing. They do not. One describes potential, the other describes actuality. Pausality makes this distinction structural rather than interpretive.

### 1.9 Relation to Existing Process Accounts of Measurement

The attempt to connect Whitehead's metaphysics to quantum measurement is not new. The present proposal should be located within an existing conversation, and its specific contribution identified.

Henry Stapp has argued over several decades that quantum events are Whiteheadian actual occasions and that conscious observation plays a constitutive role in the transition from indeterminate to determinate. Stapp's work deserves credit for taking the Whitehead-physics connection seriously when most process philosophers did not. However, his identification of measurement with *conscious* observation reintroduces precisely the privileged observer that a relational ontology should dissolve. If all occasions prehend — if experience is a feature of every actual entity, as Whitehead's panpsychism requires — then singling out consciousness as the trigger for determination imports a hierarchy among occasions that the system does not support. Consciousness is not metaphysically special. It is complex. Pausality avoids this move entirely: any downstream occasion that creates relational demand triggers satisfaction, whether or not that occasion is conscious in any recognizable sense.

Michael Epperson's *Quantum Mechanics and the Philosophy of Alfred North Whitehead* represents the most sustained attempt to map quantum formalism onto Whitehead's categories, using the decoherence framework to give rigorous content to the transition from potential to actual. Epperson's work is sophisticated and largely compatible with the present proposal. Where it stops short is on the question of the trigger. Epperson describes concrescence as selecting from among decoherent possibilities, but does not specify what determines *when* that selection occurs — what causes an occasion to reach satisfaction at this moment rather than continuing to concresce. Pausality proposes the missing mechanism: satisfaction is not an internal threshold but a relational event. The occasion completes when a downstream occasion requires the result. This is not a rejection of Epperson's framework but a completion of it — providing the trigger condition his account leaves open.

Abner Shimony, working at the intersection of philosophy of physics and process thought, proposed that actualization occurs at a threshold of physical complexity — that systems become determinate when they reach a certain level of organization. Shimony deserves recognition for insisting that actualization needs a trigger condition, a question that much of the process-physics literature leaves unaddressed. His specific proposal, however, is unsatisfying: "complexity" is a placeholder rather than an explanation, and it reintroduces the question of why *this* threshold rather than another. Pausality, or relational satisfaction, offers a structurally different kind of answer — not a threshold property of the system itself, but a relational condition between occasions. Determination is not something a system achieves internally. It is something that happens *between* occasions when one requires the result of another.
