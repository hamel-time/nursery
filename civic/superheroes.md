## The Waiting Is The Hardest Part

By Ryan Hamel

**Superheroes train you to wait.**

## What Are Stories Anyway?

Stories teach lessons.

Lessons build *mental models* in your brain.

Mental models take in a new situation and the output is an intuition for how you should behave.

We teach people stories because the universe is constantly confronting us with unique situations that we do not have an exact rule to apply. Given how many new situations we are all facing everyday, the importance of which stories we tell and which we listen to is more important than ever.

Let us take a story deeply embedded in western canon - *The Boy Who Cried Wolf*. The lesson from this story is simple: you lie about some type of danger to trigger a social response mechanism meant to defend against it, you weaken the exact social response mechanism that is required when you actually experience said danger. Cry for the wolf to get the townspeople to come and help you get the sheep in quickly (and make your job easier) and you remove the social security net that you actually need when the wolf comes for real.

Now, assuming you are not an adolescent sheepherder, it is unlikely that this exact situation arises too often in your life. So the lesson is not about sheep farming, really, it is about the danger of misusing people's goodwill to solve social problems. 

Stories deliver lessons, and lessons create mental models for handling new situations.

## What Lessons Do Superheroes Teach?
I think superheroes are stories we tell to comfort the hopeless.

We all know the setup of the typical superhero story. The world is in trouble. Humanity does not have the tools to solve it. The only one who does is a being who is literally *more than human*. They are, by definition, *something the listener can never become*.

Whatever heroism is contained within that story, it is literally beyond the capabilities of the listener, because they are **merely human** - and the heroism is performed by a **superhuman**. The lesson for the listener - the mental model that helps them predict what they should do when confronted with a similar situation - is to do nothing.

When the world is in trouble, and humanity is failing to solve it, the lesson that superheroes teach you...

is to wait.

**Superheroes train you to wait.**

## Story Hygiene

If stories have this type of power, then it is important that we be conscientious of the stories we listen to and tell. Every other day there is another superhero movie or show, or the hundredth iteration of a well-trodden path, or yet another merger of the companies that create the stories we consume.

Perhaps, then, we can be mindful of the next movie we watch, or the next show. Instead of a superhero, I suggest a powerful and compassionate storyteller who always left his listeners with a new mental model to handle the world as it comes to them - Mister Rogers. *A Beautiful Day In The Neighborhood* (2019) is a wonderful film for viewers too old for the original show which is amazing for the target audience.

Not all superhero stories are equal - *X-Men*, for instance, are rarely about individual power saving the day, but about what a group of outcasts can survive together. And, of course, there is nothing wrong with consuming them - just be mindful of the lesson being taught so that you can decide if that is a lesson worth keeping.

**A Note On Dreams**

Humans are storytellers, not just consumers. Even if you have never stood in front of an audience and told a story, each night - or perhaps during the day - you write a new story in your dreams. These dreams are a blend or a variation of stories we already know, with perhaps your own personal twist.

The consequence of that is simple: if you keep hearing the same stories, you will keep having the same dreams.

So listen for when someone tells us a new story, because new stories offer new dreams.

*To dream is to be human - to dream anew is to be human anew.*

## Redefining Superhuman

What if we changed our mental model on what a superhuman was? Instead of thinking that the superpower that solves the problem comes from the unique abilities of one person, we started seeing it as the emergent power of a group?

I think superhero stories have us trapped. They train us to look for power in the wrong place: **the within, instead of the with.**

Superhuman, to me, is what we become when we are together. When we share common goals, when we bond over a shared existence, and when something new emerges from the group that was impossible by any individual.

We are a social species that is stronger when we are bonded. Over and over again our species has shown that from many we can emerge something new - something built on special relationships, allowing our human abilities to emerge to be something greater than whatever special abilities of any individual.

So becoming superhuman, to me, does not mean someone who is above humanity, or beyond humanity - becoming superhuman is what happens when we finally stop acting like a human, and start acting like humanity.

There are no special people, only special relationships.

# Afterword

When you think of the stories that our children will tell, or their children's children and so on - what lessons do you think they will learn? To wait for someone else to save the world?

Now, imagine the stories you want your descendants to hear about what we did with our turns around the sun - what lessons do you want them to learn from what we do?  

What stores do you need to begin telling, and listening to, so that you can they will hear stories of us that we can be proud of.

If you want one place to start - spend more time with the people closest to you - family, friends and neighbours. When the crisis comes, those bonds are where the super powers come from.
